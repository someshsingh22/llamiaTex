
\section{Introduction}
\vspace{-2mm}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed to collaborate with domain
agents to solve tasks that demand their world knowledge and
domain-specific expertise respectively. 
Today this collaboration with domain agents is primarily mediated through language: the LLM invokes another agent, receives a
natural language \emph{verbalization} of its output, and reasons over that
description to decide subsequent
actions~\cite{yao2022react,schick2023toolformerlanguagemodelsteach}.
Such verbalized collaboration is effective when both agents are themselves language models, as they share the same input space. However, in many domains such as game playing, robotics, autonomous driving the
strongest available agents are not language models but pretrained neural networks whose expertise
is encoded in their internal representations: latent states that encode the agent's policy, value estimation, and learned features~\cite{silver2017mastering,monroe2024mastering,brohan2023rt2}. Verbalized collaboration forces these representations through a lossy bottleneck, collapsing the agent's rich internal state into surface-level descriptions. This raises a fundamental question:
\begin{quote}
\emph{How can LLMs effectively collaborate and jointly reason with pretrained non-language agents?}    
\end{quote}

As LLMs increasingly serve as general purpose orchestrators, the depth of their collaboration with domain experts determines what these systems can ultimately achieve. Effective collaboration has the potential to unlock creative applications that neither agent achieves alone.
Consider chess: LLMs have been trained on more chess literature than most experts will study in a lifetime, yet they cannot leverage it to play the game competently, trailing far behind even amateur players~\cite{XXXChessBench}. Conversely, pretrained engines surpassed human grandmasters decades ago~\cite{campbell2002deep}, yet they remain narrow specialists that are unable to explain the rationale behind a move or strategize under different contexts. The most valued applications commentating on a game, preparing against an opponent's known weaknesses, assessing puzzle creativity therefore remain unsolved, because they demand both the engine's deep positional understanding and the LLM's ability to reason over human intent in natural language. Improving how LLMs collaborate with domain agents would unlock these applications and make them scalable.


Earlier approaches aimed to solve these tasks by training dedicated models per task~\cite{jhamtani-etal-2018-learning,mcilroy2020aligning,mcilroy2021detecting}. While these remain strong baselines, each model is applicable only to the task it was trained on and does not transfer to new settings. To overcome this limitation of generalization, subsequent works attempt fine-tuning LLMs on interleaved language and verbalized policy tokens like next actions and value estimates in natural language ~\cite{feng2024chessgpt}. While this grounds the model in domain-specific behavior, these methods are compute inefficient and lag far behind pretrained domain experts. Another line of work instantiates domain agents through verbalization~\cite{lee2022improving,zang2019automated,kim2024bridging}; however this collapses the agent's rich latent representations into surface-level tokens such as next-best actions. These lines of work share a common limitation: the multi agent collaboration fails to effectively leverage the rich latent representations of domain agents. Prior multi-agent RL research has addressed similar information loss by co-training agents to share representations~\cite{sukhbaatar2016learning,jiang2018learning,das2019tarmac}, but in the LLM setting both systems are independently pretrained and co-training at pretraining scale is impractical. These limitations are further compounded because of the multi-step settings in these tasks.\somesh{Last sentence needs slight elaboration}

In this work we introduce the concept of \textit{latent state internalization} to bridge this gap. Rather than verbalizing the domain agent's output, we inject its internal representations directly into the LLM's chain of thought as contiguous \textit{latent tokens}. A lightweight projection module \textbf{LatentBridge}, projects these representations into the LLM's embedding space. This enables the model to reason autoregressively over both agents' latent space simultaneously. \somesh{Open to alternate name suggestions for LatentBridge}

\ref{XXXfig1} describes the multi-step flow. At each step the LLM (1) generates language to reason verbally, (2) updates the environment with actions, or (3) queries the domain agent. At each query the domain agent processes the updated state and its latent state is projected as $K=32$ contiguous latent tokens. These latent tokens are appended to the reasoning trace giving the LLM a growing record of the agent's evaluations as the multi step interaction progresses. This creates a closed loop: the agent operates inside the generation process, updating its perspective after each action. We train on these interleaved traces of \lang{language}, \action{action}, and \state{latent tokens} with reinforcement learning, enabling the model to collaborate strategically with the domain agent.

We instantiate this framework as \textbf{LLAMIA} (\textbf{L}arge \textbf{L}anguage and \textbf{A}ction \textbf{M}odels with \textbf{I}nternal \textbf{A}gents), pairing [LLM backbone] with Leela Chess Zero's BT4 network (240M parameters), and evaluate on \textsc{LLAMIA-Bench}---our curated benchmark spanning [XXX] public benchmarks and we extend it to in-the-wild settings with [XXX] tasks. This spans [XXX] tasks including behavior cloning across skill levels, behavioral stylometry, difficulty estimation, move and game commentary, and conditional planning. We ablate our method against dedicated task experts, frontier reasoning models and RL trained baselines with access to the domain agent. LLAMIA matches or exceeds all task experts and baselines with a single model, with [XXX\%] gains on [XXX]. We observe that the margin grows for tasks requiring deeper collaboration and multi-step interactions like game level commentary and conditional planning. Commentary produced through internalization shows highest overlaps with the lines discussed by grandmasters. While imitating gameplay we observe that LLAMIA even imitates tricks and blunders akin to humans under time pressure. RL with LLAMIA shows quicker and substantially higher performance gains. Our baselines and experiments conclusively prove the bottleneck of verbalization even when controlled for compute and data.

Beyond task performance, we adopt activation patching introduced by \cite{jenner2024evidence} to interpret and analyze the evolving policy and internal representations of the domain agent throughout the LLM's chain of thought. We find intriguing evidence that the policy and latent states adapt dynamically based on the target task instructions. When imitating gameplay under time pressure, squares around the king's position, and forcing lines are prioritized. This shows that the internalized representations are themselves interpretable and used to guide the LLM's reasoning.

% P4: Our approach

% These observations motivate \textit{latent state internalization} \somesh{No, first of all this is a novel concept, we need to moitvate why this is needed and what it is very clearly}: rather than communicating \somesh{communicating trivializes it, use verbalizing as text}with heterogeneous agents through text, we project their internal representations directly into the LLM's chain of thought. A lightweight adapter $H_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{K \times e}$ ({<}1\% additional parameters) maps the agent's latent state into $K{=}32$ continuous tokens in the LLM's embedding space. These tokens are interleaved with language and action tokens during autoregressive generation. Crucially, an \texttt{<InvokeAgent>} mechanism enables \textit{dynamic re-encoding}: when the LLM generates an action, the environment transitions, the agent re-encodes the new state, and fresh latent tokens are appended to the reasoning trace. This closed loop supports iterative planning over evolving states without external API calls. We train these augmented reasoning traces with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), enabling the model to learn \textit{when} to invoke the agent, \textit{which} configuration to use (routing between agents of different search depths), and \textit{how} to condition its reasoning on the resulting latent tokens.
