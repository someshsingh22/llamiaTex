
\section{Introduction}
\vspace{-2mm}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed to collaborate with domain
agents to solve tasks that demand their world knowledge and
domain-specific expertise respectively. Yet their collaboration with domain agents remains bottlenecked by language~\cite{wu2023autogen,agentverseXXX}.
Concretely, the LLM reasons through a chain of thought (CoT) that interleaves its own deliberation with the outputs of agents it invokes~\cite{yao2022react,schick2023toolformerlanguagemodelsteach}. The LLM reasons these interleaved language and actions to plan and reason further.
Such \emph{verbalized} collaboration is effective when both agents are themselves language models, as they share the same input space. However, in many domains such as game playing, robotics, autonomous driving the
strongest available agents are not language models but pretrained neural networks whose expertise
is encoded in their internal representations: latent states that encode the agent's policy, value estimation, and learned features~\cite{silver2017mastering,monroe2024mastering,brohan2023rt2}. Text-mediated collaboration forces these representations through a lossy verbalization bottleneck, collapsing the agent's rich internal state into surface-level descriptions. This raises a fundamental question:
\begin{quote}
\emph{How can LLMs effectively collaborate and jointly reason with pretrained non-language agents?}    
\end{quote}

As LLMs increasingly serve as general purpose orchestrators, the depth of their collaboration with domain experts determines what these systems can ultimately achieve. Effective collaboration has the potential to unlock creative applications that neither agent achieves alone.
Consider chess: LLMs have been trained on more chess literature than most experts will study in a lifetime, yet they cannot leverage it to play the game competently, trailing far behind even amateur players~\cite{XXXChessBench}. Conversely, pretrained engines surpassed human grandmasters decades ago~\cite{campbell2002deep}, yet they remain narrow specialists that are unable to explain the rationale behind a move or strategize under different contexts. The most valued applications commentating on a game, preparing against an opponent's known weaknesses, assessing puzzle creativity therefore remain unsolved, because they demand both the engine's deep positional understanding and the LLM's ability to reason over human intent in natural language. Improving how LLMs collaborate with domain agents would unlock these applications and make them scalable.


Earlier approaches aimed to solve these tasks by training dedicated models per task~\cite{jhamtani-etal-2018-learning,mcilroy2020aligning,mcilroy2021detecting}. While these remain strong baselines, each model is applicable only to the task it was trained on and does not transfer to new settings. To overcome this limitation of generalization, subsequent works attempt fine-tuning LLMs on interleaved language and verbalized policy tokens like next actions and value estimates in natural language ~\cite{feng2024chessgpt}. While this grounds the model in domain-specific behavior, these methods are compute inefficient and lag far behind pretrained domain experts. More recently, the rise of LLMs as general-purpose orchestrators has fueled interest in multi-agent systems~\cite{lee2022improving,zang2019automated,kim2024bridging}; however, this collaboration is mediated entirely through language, collapsing the agent's rich latent representations into surface-level tokens such as next-best actions. These lines of work share a common limitation: the multi agent collaboration fails to effectively leverage the rich latent representations of domain agents. Prior multi-agent RL research has addressed similar information loss by co-training agents to share representations~\cite{sukhbaatar2016learning,jiang2018learning,das2019tarmac}, but in the LLM setting both systems are independently pretrained and co-training at pretraining scale is impractical. These limitations are further compounded because of the multi-step settings in these tasks.

In this work we introduce the concept of \textit{latent state internalization} to bridge this gap. Rather than verbalizing the domain agent's output, we inject its internal representations directly into the LLM's chain of thought as contiguous \textit{latent tokens}. A lightweight projection module \textbf{LatentBridge}, projects these representations into the LLM's embedding space. This enables the model to reason autoregressively over both agents' latent space simultaneously. \somesh{Open to alternate name suggestions for LatentBridge}

\ref{XXXfig1} describes the multi-step flow. At each step the LLM (1) generates language to reason verbally, (2) updates the environment with actions, or (3) queries the domain agent. At each query the domain agent processes the updated state and its latent state is projected as $K=32$ contiguous latent tokens. These latent tokens are appended to the reasoning trace giving the LLM a growing record of the agent's evaluations as the multi step interaction progresses. This creates a closed loop: the agent operates inside the generation process, updating its perspective after each action. We train on these interleaved traces of \lang{language}, \action{action}, and \state{latent tokens} with reinforcement learning, enabling the model to collaborate strategically with the domain agent.

Evaluating this paradigm requires a domain with diverse collaborative tasks, verifiable metrics, and agents whose continuous representations can be extracted---a combination not previously available in a single system. Chess provides all three. Humans have collaborated with engines for decades on tasks beyond optimal play: commentary, behavioral analysis, puzzle curation, difficulty assessment, and strategic planning---all well-defined with established evaluation protocols. Neural engines like Leela Chess Zero~\cite{monroe2024mastering} produce rich continuous representations encoding positional features, piece interactions, and strategic patterns~\cite{mcgrath2022acquisition,jenner2024evidence} that can be extracted and projected into other architectures.

Internalization also enables capabilities that verbalized interfaces cannot support. Verbalized pipelines transmit only the agent's top-ranked actions, discarding its preferences across all candidates; latent tokens preserve this full output, making tasks like difficulty estimation and player-style identification accessible for the first time. Commentary produced through internalization is grounded in features the agent actually evaluates rather than hallucinated analysis; behavior cloning reproduces not just move accuracy but the characteristic error patterns of a target skill level; and instruction-conditioned planning demonstrably reshapes play in response to natural-language strategic directives. Beyond task performance, the internalized representations are themselves interpretable: a single direction in the latent space, identified via sparse autoencoders, recovers the effect of a natural-language instruction when injected without any language prompt. We validate these findings primarily in chess, with a cross-domain pilot on poker providing initial evidence of transferability.

A lightweight adapter $H_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{K \times e}$ ({<}1\% additional parameters) maps the agent's latent state into $K{=}32$ continuous tokens in the LLM's embedding space. These tokens are interleaved with language and action tokens during autoregressive generation. Crucially, an \texttt{<InvokeAgent>} mechanism enables \textit{dynamic re-encoding}: when the LLM generates an action, the environment transitions, the agent re-encodes the new state, and fresh latent tokens are appended to the reasoning trace. This closed loop supports iterative planning over evolving states without external API calls. We train these augmented reasoning traces with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), enabling the model to learn \textit{when} to invoke the agent, \textit{which} configuration to use (routing between agents of different search depths), and \textit{how} to condition its reasoning on the resulting latent tokens.

    \item \textbf{Internalization outperforms tool calling across all tasks:} LLAMIA-13B achieves 0.61 G-eval on commentary vs.\ 0.52 for GPT-4o with Lc0 tool access, and 17\% win-rate gain on instruction-conditioned planning vs.\ 10\% for LLaMA-3 405B with Lc0---while being 30$\times$ smaller (Table~\ref{tab:chess_benchmarks}).

    \item \textbf{Dynamic re-encoding is the critical mechanism:} Removing \texttt{<InvokeAgent>} causes 64\% degradation on conditional planning and 22\% on commentary, but only 1--4\% on single-step tasks (gameplay, move prediction).     \item \textbf{Both training stages are necessary:} Stage~1 (projector alignment on 50M state-policy pairs) alone achieves strong gameplay (2198 Elo) but poor language tasks (0.31 G-eval). Stage~2 without Stage~1 is unstable and plateaus 190 Elo below the full system. The staged approach leverages abundant synthetic data for stable initialization before instruction-tuning (Table~\ref{tab:stage_ablation}).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{-1mm}
\subsection{Contributions}
\vspace{-1mm}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, labelsep=0.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Latent state internalization for heterogeneous agents (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}):} We formalize and instantiate a paradigm for coupling LLMs with pretrained non-language agents by projecting their internal representations into the LLM's reasoning trace via learned adapters. Unlike tool calling, this preserves continuous policy and value information. Unlike latent multi-agent systems, this bridges architecturally heterogeneous models through a lightweight projector.

    \item \textbf{Dynamic re-encoding with \texttt{InvokeAgent} (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}):} We introduce a mechanism for iterative reasoning over evolving states: the LLM generates actions, transitions the environment, and receives fresh agent encodings mid-generation. Ablations confirm this is the critical differentiator---removing it causes 64\% degradation on multi-step planning but only 1--4\% on single-step tasks.

    \item \textbf{GRPO-trained agent-in-the-loop reasoning (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}):} We train augmented chain-of-thought traces with GRPO, enabling the model to learn optimal agent invocation strategies---including routing between multiple agent configurations of different depths. This optimizes over full reasoning rollouts containing latent tokens, rather than bare action prediction.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive empirical validation (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}):} On chess as a controlled testbed, a single LLAMIA model matches or exceeds task-specific specialists across five evaluation categories while outperforming 30$\times$ larger LLMs with tool access. We release LLAMIA-Bench for evaluating LLM-agent integration on sequential decision tasks.
\end{enumerate}
