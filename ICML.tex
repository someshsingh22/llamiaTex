\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) increasingly collaborate with agents to solve complex tasks which require domain specific decision making, planning, and reasoning. Naturally, for LLM agents this collaboration is primarily mediated through text. 
But many powerful domain-specific agents---pretrained neural networks for game playing, robotic control, or evaluation encode rich representations (policy distributions, value estimates, learned state features).

that are compressed into sparse symbolic outputs when accessed through text.

We propose \textit{latent state internalization}: projecting an agent’s internal representations directly into the LLM’s chain-of-thought reasoning via a lightweight adapter ({<}1\% additional parameters, $K{=}32$ tokens per state). An \texttt{InvokeAgent} mechanism enables dynamic re-encoding---as the LLM takes actions and the environment state evolves, the agent re-encodes the updated state and injects fresh latent tokens into the ongoing reasoning trace. We train these augmented traces with reinforcement learning (GRPO), enabling the model to learn when to invoke the agent, which agent configuration to select, and how to condition its reasoning on the resulting continuous representations.

We validate on chess---a domain with deterministic dynamics, calibrated evaluation, and superhuman pretrained agents with well-studied internal representations. A single 13B-parameter model with an internalized chess engine achieves 2279$\pm$41 Elo without search, 55\% behavior cloning accuracy across human skill levels, and 0.61 G-eval on game commentary---matching or exceeding task-specific specialists and outperforming 30$\times$ larger LLMs with conventional tool access. Ablations isolate dynamic re-encoding as the critical mechanism: removing it degrades multi-step planning by 64\% while affecting single-step predictions by only 1--4\%, establishing latent internalization as a more capable paradigm than text-based integration for coupling domain-specific agents with LLM reasoning.

\section*{Impact Statement}

Authors are \textbf{required} to include a statement of the potential broader
impact of their work, including its ethical aspects and future societal
consequences. This statement should be in an unnumbered section at the end of
the paper (co-located with Acknowledgements -- the two may appear in either
order, but both must be before References), and does not count toward the paper
page limit. In many cases, where the ethical impacts and expected societal
implications are those that are well established when advancing the field of
Machine Learning, substantial discussion is not required, and a simple
statement such as the following will suffice:

``This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field of Machine
Learning. There are many potential societal consequences of our work, none
which we feel must be specifically highlighted here.''

The above statement can be used verbatim in such cases, but we encourage
authors to think about whether there is content which does warrant further
discussion, as this statement will be apparent if the paper is later flagged
for ethics review.
